RISING SEA LEVELS

Ocean levels are rising at unprecedented rates. Due to byproducts of climate change like greenhouse gases, melting glaciers, and thermal expansion, experts worry that rising sea levels will "wreak havoc on coastal cities across the world."1

As a coastal city, Boston experiences the consequences of changes in sea level. Though the city already experiences regular flooding, Boston is predicted to face up to 1.5 feet of sea-level rise by 2050.3

Situated on the water, Boston's Seaport district is at risk of serious damage to buildings and roads as a result of future flooding. The following data visualizations outline how changes in sea level will impact flooding in Seaport.

Changes in sea level can affect many aspects of a city's ecosystem. Two common side effects of sea-level rise that may impact the Seaport neighborhood are increased flooding and heavy precipitation.

Below are visualizations that outline the past, present and future of these climate patterns in Boston.

Which trend would you like to explore?

Since the 1950s, heavy precipitation has become more common around the world. This trend will continue as the planet continues to warm, because warmer air can hold more water vapor. In the United States, the Midwest and Northeast have experienced the largest increase in frequency of heavy precipitation events. 5

The most immediate threat of heavy precipitation is flooding. In urban areas such as Boston, floods have the ability to devastate roads and infrastructure, harm human health, and overwhelm sewer systems.

1966-1996

The primary indicators for changes to precipitation rates are extreme weather events such as prolonged droughts and flash flooding. 5 These precursors are likely the cause for a rise in heavy precipitation events between 1987 and 1996, after remaining stready throughout the mid-1960s to the mid-1980s.

1997-2016

From 1997 to 2006, the average inches of precipitation per year increased even more dramatically than in the previous decade; then plateaued from 2007 to 2016. As a result, cities in the Northeast have experienced “heavy rain and snow resulting in about 1 to 2 inches of water a day depending on location since 1996.” 6

Floods are among the most common natural disasters in the United States. They can cause loss of human life, destruction of property and an increase in waterborne diseases. As global warming continues to exacerbate rises in sea level, it “detectably influences” many variables that cause flooding, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change. 1

Boston has historically surpassed national averages in annual flooding. Studies show this trend is likely to continue.

1920-1990

Early data shows Boston's location on the water may have boosted its susceptibility to flooding.

From 1920 to 1990, the number of high-tide flood days, days on which tides inundated low-lying areas, increased steadily across the nation. But Boston consistently recorded more high-tide flood days per decade than the national average.

1990-2010

In just two decades between 1990 and 2010, the number of high-tide flood days skyrocketed throughout the United States. But again, Boston faced an especially drastic spike in high-tide flooding. The number of flood days in Boston tripled from 1990 to 2010, while the national average doubled.

Much of Boston’s infrastructure is not prepared to face continuous increases in annual flood days, as it is one the United States' oldest cities. Dated drainage systems are common in Boston and are not equipped to handle repeated floods.3

2000-2050

By 2050, Boston is predicted to experience 70 high-tide flood days per year. That is more than four times as many high-tide flood days as there were from 2010-2019.

Such a drastic rise in flooding “endangers crucial infrastructure in Boston, including waste and stormwater systems, MBTA lines, and roads.” 4

Flooding and heavy precipitation events caused by rising sea levels have plagued the Boston area. Predictions show these extreme weather events will only worsen in years to come.

What do you think the future will look like?

The previous visualization shows the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, NOAA's, estimates for future sea-level rise in Boston. Even by intermediate estimates, sea levels in Boston will rise by over a foot by 2050. High estimates are even more severe and both projections only worsen with time. Both high and intermediate estimates are far above the city code for development, which defines what degree of potential flood risk is safe for infrastructure development.

Boston endures worse high-tide flooding than most cities in the United States.3 By NOAA's estimates, large portions of Boston, including Seaport, will be swallowed up by the sea in the coming century.

We hope that this project will increase the Boston community's awareness of the problem, as well as motivate further research on the effect of rising sea levels in Boston.

Project G01: Rising Sea Levels, DS 4200 S22

Mira Chadha, Michelle Lo, Annetta Stogniew

Service-Learning Course Project as part of Ds 4200 S22 Information Presentation & Visualization, taught by Prof. Cody Dunne, Data Visualization @ Khoury, Northeastern University.

Abstract

Our visual encodings show trends in flooding, heavy precipitation and sea level rise in Seaport, Boston and the Northeast United States. The visualizations were created to be shown in Boston Globe journalist David Abel’s documentary “Inundation Project,” about rising sea levels in Seaport.

Because our visual encodings will ultimately appear in a documentary, we got special permission from the professor to use Adobe Illustrator and Adobe After Effects to create our visualizations. The professor also allowed us to focus more on storytelling in our website, rather than the interactivity required in the project description.

The first two visualizations, embedded as videos, will appear as-is in Abel’s documentary. The remaining visualizations mimic those we created for Abel. We recreated the visualizations Abel will use in D3 to adapt to the website display. For instance, the D3 encodings each use different colors because they represent different datasets. For Abel’s documentary, all visualizations use the same color scheme and use the context from the documentary to advise viewers on the encoding’s content.

The motivating question for Abel’s documentary, and in turn our project, is how rising sea levels will impact the Seaport community in the coming century. Using past data and projections from NOAA and the First Street Foundation, our visualizations show how the Seaport neighborhood specifically and the broader geographic areas to which Seaport belongs will be impacted by sea level rise and its side effects.
